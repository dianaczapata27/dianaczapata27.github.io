Identification of structures in the Universe through quasar clustering



Abstract

Matter in the Universe organizes into a cosmic web of filaments and nodes, whose mapping at high redshifts requires luminous tracers. In this work we study the large-scale structure using quasars from the Quaia catalog—in its two magnitude-limited samples, \(G<20.5\) and \( G<20.0\)—as the main tracers. We implement a reproducible methodological chain that begins with the cleaning of the sample via sigma-clipping in the \((z, z_{\mathrm{error}}/z)\) plane. After this filtering, we retain \(\simeq 67.2\%\) of the original sample with \(G<20.0\) and \(\simeq 61.3\%\) of the sample with \(G<20.5\). From this selection we define the SubQuaia catalog and construct nine volume-limited samples (VLS) for each magnitude cut. From them we estimate (i) the luminosity function, (ii) the correlation function using the Landy–Szalay estimator, and (iii) a mass assignment to the dark matter halos that host the quasars. With this information we reconstruct the density field by means of the halo-based method, and extract the filamentary skeleton with DisPerSE.

We analyze the impact of spatial smoothing: greater smoothing systematically increases filament lengths, as expected when the smoothing sets the effective resolution scale. We also identify anomalies in the distances between vertices due to boundary effects. We apply a geometric filter that cuts segments with separations greater than \(30\ \mathrm{Mpc}\) and generates subfilaments, thereby stabilizing the local geometry. We compute the occupation of the filaments—defined as the number of quasars for which that filament is the nearest—and discard filaments with occupation < 5 for the samples \(M_i \le -23\) and \(M_i \le -22\), and < 3 for \(M_i \le -24\).

With the resulting set we characterize the filaments by hemispheres (Galactic North and South) by means of: the distribution of distances between consecutive vertices, the distribution of lengths, the occupation as a function of length, the cumulative distribution functions (CDF) of the lengths, and the filament density.

We identified indications of hemispheric asymmetries in these metrics; therefore, a specific overdensity analysis was carried out that included: (i) radial counts of the number of filaments; (ii) an angular sweep to locate the angular position of the overdensities on the sky; (iii) the cross-correlation function to assess the consistency between the density peaks detected in quasars and in filaments; and (iv) a visual inspection of quasar clusters around those peaks together with the filamentary skeleton.

Independently, all these measures show consistent signals of simultaneous overdensity in the distributions of quasars and filaments. These signals are not primarily associated with the position of the Magellanic Clouds; consequently, we posit the hypothesis of an underlying large-scale structure. The verification of this hypothesis and its relation to the observed overdensities is left for future work.

Finally, the choice of quasars as tracers, the halo-based field reconstruction, and the skeleton obtained with DisPerSE, constitute a viable and reproducible route to map the cosmic web in large volumes and lay the foundations for subsequent, more refined studies of its morphology.

Keywords: Cosmic web; large-scale structure; quasars; clustering; correlation function; halo-based method; density field reconstruction; DisPerSE.
